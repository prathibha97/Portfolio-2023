For the first two years of my engineering career, I had a habit I&apos;m not proud of: I&apos;d polish a demo for a week before showing it to anyone.

The work _looked_ better than the people delivering theirs. The work also wasn&apos;t in production.

## The pattern I broke

I&apos;d build something, then build the second version of it in my head before merging the first one. Refactors before the seams were even warm. Three-day side-quests to add a feature flag for a state with one user.

The flow felt productive. The output wasn&apos;t.

## What I do now

A few simple rules that compound:

- **First draft is shippable.** If it works on the happy path, merge it.
- **No abstraction without three concrete uses.** Premature abstraction is more expensive than duplication.
- **Comments only for surprises.** A `// TODO` is the most-useful comment I&apos;ve ever written.
- **Demos are screenshots, not slide decks.** If you need slides, the thing doesn&apos;t work yet.

> The compounding edge isn&apos;t writing better code. It&apos;s writing _enough_ code, sooner, and letting reality grade it.

I still polish things. But polish comes after a thing exists in the world. Not before.

## What this means for hiring

When you read about me, this is the lens to use. I optimize for **velocity-with-taste**, not for impressive single artifacts. If you need someone to live in a Figma for three months, I&apos;m the wrong hire. If you need a human who can take "we need something live by Friday" and have it staged by Wednesday, that&apos;s the work I&apos;m best at.
